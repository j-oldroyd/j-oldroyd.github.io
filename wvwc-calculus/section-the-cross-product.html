Section 10.4 The Cross Product

¶

The dot product, and in particular Theorem 10.3.5, gives us a good way to tell if two vectors are perpendicular. However, it says nothing about how to construct perpendicular vectors. The next vector operation, the cross product, is the tool we'll use for that goal.

Subsection 10.4.1 Definition and Properties of the Cross Product

¶
Definition 10.4.1. The Cross Product.

Let \(\vv{x} = \dotprod{x_{1},y_{1},z_{1}}\) and \(\vv{y} = \dotprod{x_{2},y_{2},z_{2}}\text{.}\) Then the cross product of \(\vv{x}\) with \(\vv{y}\) is the new vector \(\vv{x}\times\vv{y}\) given by

\begin{equation*} \vv{x}\times\vv{y} = \dotprod{y_{1}z_{2}-z_{1}y_{2}, z_{1}x_{2}-x_{1}z_{2}, x_{1}y_{2} - y_{1}x_{2}}. \end{equation*}
Example 10.4.2. Cross product of basis vectors.

Let's start by computing \(\vv{i}\times\vv{k}\) using the definition. If we do so, we have

\begin{equation*} \vv{i}\times\vv{k} = \dotprod{0,-1,0} = -\vv{j}. \end{equation*}

On the other hand, we also have \(\vv{k}\times\vv{i} = \vv{j}\text{.}\) This points out the very important fact that order matters for cross products.

This formula is a lot to remember, so it's beneficial to find another way to express it. One way is by using determinants. In particular, if \(\vv{x} = \dotprod{x_{1},y_{1},z_{1}}\) and \(\vv{y} = \dotprod{x_{2},y_{2},z_{2}}\text{,}\) then

\begin{equation} \vv{x}\times\vv{y} = \left|\begin{array}{ccc} \vv{i} & \vv{j} & \vv{k} \\ x_{1} & y_{1} & z_{1} \\ x_{2} & y_{2} & z_{2} \end{array}\right|\label{equation-cross-product-determinant}\tag{10.4.1} \end{equation}
Example 10.4.3. Another cross product.

(10.4.1) is useful to use if you're dealing with vectors that aren't as simple as the basis vectors \(\vv{i},\vv{j},\vv{k}\text{.}\) For example, let \(\vv{a} = \dotprod{-1,2,0}\) and \(\vv{b} = \dotprod{2,4,1}\text{.}\) Then

\begin{align*} \vv{a}\times\vv{b} & = \left|\begin{array}{ccc} \vv{i} & \vv{j} & \vv{k} \\ -1 & 2 & 0 \\ 2 & 4 & 1 \end{array}\right| \\ & = \vv{i}\left|\begin{array}{cc} 2 & 0 \\ 4 & 1\end{array}\right| - \vv{j}\left|\begin{array}{cc} -1 & 0 \\ 2 & 1\end{array}\right| + \vv{k}\left|\begin{array}{cc} -1 & 2 \\ 2 & 4\end{array}\right|\\ & = 2\vv{i}+\vv{j}-8\vv{k}\\ & = \dotprod{2,1,-8}. \end{align*}

Remember that we said the cross product is our tool for finding perpendicular vectors. So it might be nice if we made sure it actually did that. As a quick check, we'll compute \(\vv{a}\cdot(\vv{a}\times\vv{b})\) and \(\vv{b}\cdot(\vv{a}\times\vv{b})\text{,}\) with these vectors coming from (10.4.1). If we do so, we obtain

\begin{align*} \vv{a}\cdot(\vv{a}\times\vv{b}) & = -2 + 2 = 0 \\ \vv{b}\cdot(\vv{a}\times\vv{b}) & = 4 + 4 - 8 = 0 \end{align*}

Since these dot products are zero, this means that both \(\vv{a}\) and \(\vv{b}\) are perpendicular to the cross product \(\vv{a}\times\vv{b}\text{.}\) This is also true in general.

Theorem 10.4.4. Orthogonality of the Cross Product.

\(\vv{a}\times\vv{b}\) is always orthogonal to both \(\vv{a}\) and \(\vv{b}\text{.}\)

So the cross product always produces orthogonal vectors. To determine the direction of the cross product \(\vv{a}\times\vv{b}\text{,}\) we use the right-hand rule: sweep your right hand from \(\vv{a}\) to \(\vv{b}\) and stick your thumb up. Then \(\vv{a}\times\vv{b}\) is parallel to your thumb.

We would also like to know the magnitude of the cross product. We can just compute it using Definition 10.4.1 and find the magnitude using our usual formula. If we do so, we obtain (after a lot of simplifying!)

\begin{equation*} \norm{\vv{a}\times\vv{b}}^{2} = \norm{\vv{a}}^{2}\norm{\vv{b}}^{2}\sin^{2}\theta, \end{equation*}

which reduces to the following result.

Theorem 10.4.5. Magnitude of the Cross Product.

Let \(\theta\) denote the acute angle between the vectors \(\vv{a}\) and \(\vv{b}\text{,}\) so that \(0\leq\theta\leq\pi\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \norm{\vv{a}\times\vv{b}} = \norm{\vv{a}}\norm{\vv{b}}\sin\theta. \end{equation*}

So in particular, two nonzero vectors \(\vv{x}\) and \(\vv{y}\) are parallel (i.e. have \(\theta=0\)) if and only if \(\vv{a}\times\vv{b} = \vv{0}\text{.}\)

Example 10.4.6. Testing collinearity.

We say that three points \(P,Q\) and \(R\) are collinear if they all lie on the same line. Suppose we want to check if the points \(P(-2,0,1), Q(-1,2,4)\) and \(R(1,6,10)\) are collinear or not. How can we do so? If we start by defining

\begin{equation*} \vv{u} = \vec{PQ} = \dotprod{1,2,3}\quad\text{and}\quad \vv{v} = \vec{QR} = \dotprod{2,4,6}, \end{equation*}

then we can say that all three points lie on the same line if and only if \(\vv{u}\) and \(\vv{v}\) are parallel to each other. So we'll compute their cross product to get \(\vv{u}\times\vv{v} = \vv{0}\text{.}\) Since these vectors are parallel, then the three given points must lie on the same line.

Another important property of the magnitude of the cross product is the following: \(\norm{\vv{a}\times\vv{b}}\) is exactly equal to the area of the parallelogram determined by \(\vv{a}\) and \(\vv{b}\text{.}\)

Example 10.4.7. Area of a triangle.

Suppose that we want the area of the triangle with vertices \(P(1,2,-1), Q(2,0,0)\) and \(R(1,-1,-2)\text{.}\) To start, we need to find vectors that determine the triangle. We can use

\begin{equation*} \vv{u} = \vec{PQ} = \dotprod{1,-2,1}\quad\text{and}\quad\vv{v} = \vec{PR} = \dotprod{0,-3,-1}. \end{equation*}

Now, the triangle determined by \(\vv{u}\) and \(\vv{v}\) is precisely half of the parallelogram determined by these same vectors, so the area of this triangle is equal to \(\frac{1}{2}\norm{\vv{u}\times\vv{v}}\text{.}\) We can use Sage as in the cell below to find the cross product of these vectors. Doing so, we get \(\vv{u}\times\vv{v} = \dotprod{5,1,-3}.\) So the area of the triangle with vertices \(P,Q\) and \(R\) is

\begin{align*} \operatorname{area} \triangle PQR & = \frac{1}{2}\norm{\vv{u}\times\vv{v}} \\ & = \frac{1}{2}\norm{\dotprod{5,1,-3}} \\ & = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{35}. \end{align*}
Theorem 10.4.8. Properties of the Cross Product.

Let \(\vv{a},\vv{b},\vv{c}\) be vectors and \(c\) a scalar. Then the following properties are true:

  1. \(\displaystyle \vv{a}\times\vv{b} = -\vv{b}\times\vv{a}\)
  2. \(\displaystyle (c\vv{a})\times\vv{b} = c(\vv{a}\times\vv{b}) = \vv{a}\times(c\vv{b})\)
  3. \(\displaystyle \vv{a}\times(\vv{b}+\vv{c}) = \vv{a}\times\vv{b}+\vv{a}\times\vv{c}\)
  4. \(\displaystyle (\vv{a}+\vv{b})\times\vv{c} = \vv{a}\times\vv{c}+\vv{b}\times\vv{c}\)
  5. \(\displaystyle \vv{a}\cdot(\vv{b}\times\vv{c}) = (\vv{a}\times\vv{b})\cdot\vv{c}\)

The last item above is an important relationship between the cross product and dot product called the scalar triple product. There is an important geometric significance to this new product.

Theorem 10.4.9. Geometry of the Scalar Triple Product.

Let \(\vv{a},\vv{b}\) and \(\vv{c}\) be vectors in \(\RR^{3}\text{.}\) Then \(|\vv{a}\cdot(\vv{b}\times\vv{c})|\) is equal to the volume of the parallelepiped determined by \(\vv{a},\vv{b}\) and \(\vv{c}\text{.}\)

Example 10.4.10. Testing if vectors are coplanar.

We say that three vectors \(\vv{u},\vv{v}\) and \(\vv{w}\) are coplanar if they can all lie in a single plane. For example, \(\vv{i},\vv{k}\) and \(\dotprod{-4,0,56}\) are coplanar since they lie in the \(xz\)-plane, but \(\vv{i},\vv{j}\) and \(\vv{k}\) are not coplanar. Suppose we're given \(\vv{u} = \dotprod{-3,2,1},\vv{v} = \dotprod{1,2,0}\) and \(\vv{w} = \dotprod{1,1,1}\text{.}\) These vectors are coplanar if and only if the parallelepiped determined by these vectors has zero volume (i.e. is flat). Since

\begin{equation*} \vv{u}\cdot(\vv{v}\times\vv{w}) = -9, \end{equation*}

these vectors are not coplanar.

Example 10.4.11. Another way to compute cross products.

Using Theorem 10.4.8 and the facts that

\begin{align*} \vv{i}\times\vv{j} & = \vv{k} \\ \vv{j}\times\vv{k} & = \vv{i} \\ \vv{k}\times\vv{i} & = \vv{j} \\ \vv{a}\times\vv{a} & = \vv{0} \end{align*}

gives us another way to compute cross products that doesn't involve determinants. As an example, let \(\vv{u} = \dotprod{1,4,-3}\) and \(\vv{v} = \dotprod{0,2,1}.\) Then

\begin{align*} \vv{u}\times\vv{v} & = (\vv{i}+4\vv{j}-3\vv{k})\times(2\vv{j}+\vv{k}) \\ & = 2\vv{i}\times\vv{j} + \vv{i}\times\vv{k} +8\vv{j}\times\vv{j} + 4\vv{j}\times\vv{k} - 6\vv{k}\times\vv{j} -3\vv{k}\times\vv{k} \\ & = 2\vv{k}-\vv{j}+4\vv{i}-6\vv{i} \\ & = \dotprod{-2,-1,2}. \end{align*}

Subsection 10.4.2 Torque

¶

Consider a force \(\vv{F}\) acting on a rigid body at some position \(\vv{r}\text{.}\) This force applies a turning affect to the body, that we measure by torque.

Definition 10.4.12. Torque.

The torque \(\boldsymbol{\tau}\) of a force \(\vv{F}\) acting at a position \(\vv{r}\) is defined to be the vector

\begin{equation*} \boldsymbol{\tau} = \vv{r}\times\vv{F}. \end{equation*}

As one example of torque, consider a wrench applied to a bolt. The force is exerted at the end of the wrench, and the torque is a vector that's parallel to the axis of rotation of the bolt. The torque is greater if the force is applied at a direction perpendicular (or nearly so) to that of the wrench, and smaller if the force is nearly parallel to the direction of the wrench. As a quick check, the torque is \(\vv{0}\) if the force is exactly parallel to the direction of the wrench, which makes sense: if we're pushing or pulling the wrench, the bolt won't rotate at all.

Example 10.4.13. Torque and hex keys.

A hex key (Allen wrench) with a short arm of length 27 mm and a long arm length of 154 mm is applied to a screw, with the short arm attached to the screw. To turn the screw, a force of 0.5 N is applied to the long arm of the hex key turning the screw clockwise, and is exactly perpendicular to both the short arm and long arm of the hex key. We want to find the torque of this force on the screw.

One way we can do this is to imagine the screw sitting at the origin, and the hex key is (initially) in the \(xy\)-plane. Note that once the force is applied, it will begin to rotate the hex key out of the \(xy\)-plane. Now, the torque is defined by

\begin{equation*} \boldsymbol{\tau} = \vv{r}\times\vv{F} \end{equation*}

where \(\vv{r}\) is the vector from the screw to the point where the force \(\vv{F}\) is applied. We can find \(\vv{r}\) without too much trouble: it's \(\vv{r} = \dotprod{.027,.154,0}\text{.}\) To find the force \(\vv{F}\text{,}\) note that it's perpendicular to both the long arm and short arm of the screw. So a starting guess would be

\begin{equation*} \vv{F} = \dotprod{.027,0,0}\times\dotprod{0,.154,0} = \dotprod{0,0,.042}. \end{equation*}

Such a force would turn the screw clockwise, but it has the wrong magnitude. So we need to adjust it a bit: \(\vv{F} = \dotprod{0,0,.5}\text{.}\) So the torque is given by

\begin{align*} \mathbf{\tau} &= \vv{r}\times\vv{F} \\ & = \dotprod{.027,.154,0}\times\dotprod{0,0,.5} \\ & = \dotprod{.154,-.027,0}. \end{align*}
