Section 8.5 Area of a Surface of Revolution

Consider a function \(y=f(x)\text{.}\) We generate a solid of revolution by rotating the graph of \(f(x)\) from \(x=a\) to \(x=b\) about the \(x\)-axis. We want to find the surface area of this figure. We can't find it directly yet, but we can approximate it by using conical frustums.

The surface area of a conical frustum is given by the formula

\begin{equation*} S_{F}=\pi(R_{1}+R_{2})\sqrt{(\Delta x)^{2}+(\Delta y)^{2}}=\pi(R_{1}+R_{2})\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}\right)^{2}}\Delta x. \end{equation*}

If \(\Delta x\approx0\) then we can say

\begin{equation*} S_{F}\approx2\pi f(x)\sqrt{1+f'(x)^{2}}\Delta x \end{equation*}

for some \(f(x)\) between \(R_{1}\) and \(R_{2}\text{.}\)

By using multiple frustums, we can approximate the surface area of the solid. Break \([a,b]\) into \(n\) subintervals

\begin{equation*} [x_{0},x_{1}],[x_{1},x_{2}],\ldots,[x_{n-1},x_{n}] \end{equation*}

with \(x_{0}=a\) and \(x_{n}=b\text{,}\) where each subinterval has length \(\Delta x\text{.}\) Then if \(S\) is the surface area of the solid, we have

\begin{align*} S\amp\approx\text{sum of areas of frustums}\\ \amp\approx\sum_{k=1}^{n}2\pi f(x_{i})\sqrt{1+f'(x_{i})^{2}}\Delta x. \end{align*}

This approximation should become more exact as \(\Delta x \to 0\text{,}\) so we have

\begin{equation*} S=\lim_{\Delta x\to0}\sum_{k=1}^{n}2\pi f(x_{i})\sqrt{1+f'(x_{i})^{2}}\Delta x \end{equation*}

or

\begin{equation*} S=\int_{a}^{b}2\pi f(x)\sqrt{1+f'(x)^{2}}\,dx. \end{equation*}

Similarly, if we generate a solid of revolution by rotating \(x=g(y)\) from \(y=c\) to \(y=d\) about the \(y\)-axis, the surface area of this solid is given by

\begin{equation*} S=\int_{c}^{d}2\pi g(y)\sqrt{1+g'(y)^{2}}\,dy. \end{equation*}

Example 8.5.1. Finding Surface Area of a Sphere.

Find the surface area of a sphere of radius \(r\text{.}\)

Solution.

We can create a sphere of radius \(r\) by rotating the semicircle \(y=\sqrt{r^{2}-x^{2}}\) about the \(x\)-axis, from \(x=-r\) to \(x=r\text{.}\) So \(f(x)=\sqrt{r^{2}-x^{2}}\) and \(f'(x)=\displaystyle\frac{-x}{\sqrt{r^{2}-x^{2}}}\text{.}\) So the surface area is

\begin{equation*} S=\int_{-r}^{r}2\pi\sqrt{r^{2}-x^{2}}\sqrt{1+\frac{x^{2}}{r^{2}-x^{2}}}\,dx \end{equation*}

or just

\begin{equation*} S=\int_{-r}^{r}2\pi r \, dx. \end{equation*}

Example 8.5.2.

Find the surface area of the solid generated by rotating the graph of \(y=x^{2}\) from \(y=0\) to \(y=3\) about the \(y\)-axis.

Solution.

Since we are rotating about the \(y\)-axis, we need to use the other formula. Since \(y=x^{2}\text{,}\) we can rewrite this as \(x=sqrt{y}\text{.}\) So the surface area is

\begin{equation*} \int_{0}^{3}2\pi\sqrt{y}\sqrt{1+\frac{1}{4y}}\,dy. \end{equation*}

Example 8.5.3.

Find the integral that gives the surface area of the solid generated by rotating the graph of \(y=x^{2}\) from \(x=0\) to \(x=3\) about the \(x\)-axis.

Solution.

By the surface area formula, the surface area is

\begin{equation*} \int_{0}^{3}2 \pi x^{2} \sqrt{1+4x^{2}}\,dx. \end{equation*}

Example 8.5.4. Gabriel's Horn.

Find the volume of the solid of revolution obtained by rotating the graph of \(y = \frac{1}{x}\) about the \(x\)-axis from \(x = 1\) onwards. Estimate the surface area of this solid of revolution.

Solution.

The volume is given by

\begin{equation*} \int_{1}^{\infty}\frac{\pi}{x^{2}}\,dx = \pi\text{.} \end{equation*}

The integral that gives the surface area is

\begin{align*} \int_{1}^{\infty}\frac{2\pi}{x}\sqrt{1 + \frac{1}{x^{2}}}\,dx \amp \gt \int_{1}^{\infty}\frac{2\pi}{x}\,dx \\ \amp = \infty \end{align*}

Oddly enough, the volume of this region is finite, but the surface area is infinite. A silly way to describe this situation: you can fill this region with a finite amount of paint, but it would take an infinite amount to paint the surface.

Example 8.5.5.

Find the surface area of the solid generated by rotating the graph of \(y=x^{3}\) from \(x=1\) to \(x=2\) about the \(x\)-axis.

Solution.

Since \(f(x)=x^{3}\text{,}\) then \(f'(x)=3x^{2}\) and the surface area of the solid is

\begin{equation*} \int_{1}^{2}2\pi x^{3}\sqrt{1+9x^{4}},dx. \end{equation*}
