Consider \(f(x) = x^{2}\text{.}\) If we graph this, we get a parabola. What we'd like to do is to find a way to describe how quickly this parabola is changing at a point, i.e. find the "slope" of the parabola. One way to try to deal with this is to use secant lines. Recall that the secant line through the points \((a,f(a))\) and \((b,f(b))\) has slope \(\frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b-a}\text{,}\) which is just the average rate of change of \(f\) from \(x=a\) to \(x=b\text{.}\) If \(b\) is very close to \(a\text{,}\) then the slope of the secant line through these points should be a good approximation to the "slope" of \(f(x) = x^{2}\) at the point \(x=a\text{.}\)
