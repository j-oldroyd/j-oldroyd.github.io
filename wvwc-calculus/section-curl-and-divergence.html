Section 14.5 Curl and Divergence

In this section we look at two different analogues of the derivative for vector fields. Once we have these versions of the derivative, we'll also be able to state a corresponding version of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, just as the Fundamental Theorem of Line Integrals corresponds to the gradient.

Subsection 14.5.1 Curl

In Section 14.3 we introduced the \(\del\) operator, which in \(\RR^{3}\) is given by

\begin{equation*} \del = \dotprod{\pdv{}{x}, \pdv{}{y}, \pdv{}{z}}\text{.} \end{equation*}

In this class, \(\del\) has no meaning when by itself. However, it gains meaning when “multiplied” by a scalar function \(f\text{,}\) leaving the gradient \(\grad f\text{.}\) We also used it to define the curl, which we repeat below.

Definition 14.5.1. Curl of a Vector Field in \(\RR^{3}\).

Let \(\vb{F} = P\vb{i} + Q\vb{j} + R\vb{k}\) be a vector field in \(\RR^{3}\) with continuously differentiable components. The curl of \(\vb{F}\) is the vector

\begin{equation*} \curlt{\vb{F}} = \curl\vb{F} = \parens{\pdv{R}{y} - \pdv{Q}{z}}\vb{i} + \parens{\pdv{P}{z} - \pdv{R}{x}}\vb{j} + \parens{\pdv{Q}{x} - \pdv{P}{y}}\vb{k}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We've already seen a couple nice properties of curls. First, the curl vector gives the axis of rotation about which \(\vb{F}\) tends to rotate counterclockwise when viewed head on, and \(\norm{\curl\vb{F}}\) is a measure of the amount of rotation. Second, the curl can be used to determine if a vector field is conservative or not.

Theorem 14.5.2. Conservative Vector Fields in \(\RR^{3}\).

Let \(\vb{F}\) denote a continuously differentiable vector field on an open set containing the simply connected region \(D\text{.}\) Then \(\vb{F}\) is conservative if and only if \(\curl\vb{F} = \vb{0}\text{.}\)

Example 14.5.3. Determining Rotation in a Vector Field.

Suppose the vector field \(\vb{F} = \dotprod{1, \sin z, x - yz^{2}}\) represents a swirling fluid. Within this fluid you place a small paddle wheel at the point \((1,-2,0)\text{.}\) When viewed from directly above, will the paddle wheel tend to rotate clockwise or counterclockwise?

Solution.

First we need to get a measure of the rotation of \(\vb{F}\text{,}\) so we compute the curl:

\begin{equation*} \curl\vb{F} = \dotprod{-z^{2} - \cos z, -1, 0}\text{,} \end{equation*}

which is just \(\dotprod{-1, -1, 0}\) at our point. This provides the axis around which the paddle wheel rotates counterclockwise. And since \(\curl\vb{F}\cdot\vb{k} = 0\text{,}\) the paddle wheel does not appear to rotate at all when viewed from above.

Subsection 14.5.2 Divergence

Our second notion of derivative for vector fields is the divergence. If \(\vb{F}\) is a continuously differentiable vector field, then we define the divergence of \(\vb{F}\) to be the scalar function \(\divt\vb{F}\) given by

\begin{equation*} \divt\vb{F} = \div\vb{F}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The divergence of a vector field is a measure of “outflow minus inflow”. If \(\div\vb{F} = 0\text{,}\) then we say that \(\vb{F}\) is incompressible or solenoidal (just as we say that \(\vb{F}\) is irrotational if \(\curl\vb{F} = \vb{0}\)).

Example 14.5.4. Divergence on a Rectangle.

Let \(\vb{F} = \dotprod{xy, x}\) and let \(C\) denote the unit circle, traversed counterclockwise once. Find the divergence of \(\vb{F}\) at \((-1,0)\) and \((0,-1)\text{.}\) Then find the net divergence of \(\vb{F}\) through the interior of \(C\text{.}\)

Solution.

At \((-1,0)\text{,}\) \(\div\vb{F} = y = 0\text{.}\) At this particular point, outflow is balanced with inflow. Likewise, at \((0,-1)\) we can see that inflow is greater than outflow.

We can compute the net divergence as \(\iint_{D}\div\vb{F}\,dA\text{,}\) where \(D\) is the interior of the unit circle. This is equal to \(0\text{,}\) which means the net flow throughout \(D\) is \(0\text{.}\) By Theorem 14.4.5, this is also equal to the net flux across \(C\text{.}\)

Subsection 14.5.3 Laplacian

From the divergence we get another useful form of (second) derivative for scalar functions. First, let \(f(x,y,z)\) be a differentiable scalar function. Then we can compute its gradient \(\grad f\text{.}\) This is a vector field that represents how \(f\) changes. Now, since \(\grad f\) is a vector field we can also consider its curl and divergence. If \(f\) is “nice enough” we know that \(\curl(\grad f) = \vb{0}\text{,}\) which is not particularly useful in this case. But if we take the divergence, we get

\begin{equation*} \div(\grad f) = f_{xx} + f_{yy} + f_{zz}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Definition 14.5.5. Laplacian.

Let \(f\) be a twice differentiable scalar function on \(\RR^{3}\text{.}\) The Laplacian of \(f\) is the function

\begin{equation*} \div(\grad f) = \del^{2}f = \Delta f \end{equation*}

given by \(f_{xx} + f_{yy} + f_{zz}\text{.}\)

Definition 14.5.5 extends to other dimensions in the obvious way. The Laplacian is useful since it provides a measure of how a function's value at a point differs from the average value at nearby points.

Example 14.5.6. Laplacians and Average Values.

Let \(f_{1} = x^{2} + y^{2}\) and let \(f_{2} = x^{2} - y^{2}\text{.}\) Let \(C\) denote the unit circle traversed once counterclockwise. Find the average values of \(f_{i}\) on \(C\text{,}\) their specific values at \((0,0)\) and the Laplacians at \((0,0)\text{.}\)

Solution.

If we compute the average values, we get

\begin{align*} \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint_{C}f_{1}(x,y)\,ds \amp = 1 \\ \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint_{C}f_{2}(x,y)\,ds \amp = 0 \text{.} \end{align*}

Furthermore, \(f_{1}(0,0) = 0 = f_{2}(0,0)\text{.}\) We also have \(\Delta f_{1} = 4\) and \(\Delta f_{2} = 0\text{.}\)

To see how the Laplacian can arise, consider the following situation explained in Evans' Partial Differential Equations, \(2^{\text{nd}}\) edition. We have some density function \(f\text{;}\) this could be mass density, charge density, etc. Now we'll let \(\vb{F}\) denote the flux of \(f\text{.}\) It's often reasonable to assume that the flux is proportional to the negative of the gradient of \(f\text{:}\) \(\vb{F} = -a\grad f\) where \(a \gt 0\text{.}\) This means that the quantity flows from regions of higher concentration to regions of lower concentration. If \(f\) represents a quantity in equilibrium within some region \(D\text{,}\) then the net flux across \(D\) should be \(0\text{.}\) In terms of Theorem 14.4.5, we have

\begin{equation*} \iint_{D}\div\vb{F}\,dA = \oint_{\partial D}\vb{F}\cdot\vb{n}\,ds = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

Now we'll make the argument that \(\div\vb{F} = 0\) since the above should be true for arbitrary subregions of \(D\text{.}\) But

\begin{equation*} \div\vb{F} = -a\div(\grad f) = -a\del^{2}f\text{,} \end{equation*}

which means that \(\Delta f = 0\text{.}\)

We say that a function is harmonic if its Laplacian is \(0\text{.}\) The equation \(\Delta f = 0\) is known as Laplace's equation. Harmonic functions are extremely useful, as they represent quantities in a kind of equilibrium state. If \(f\) represents chemical concentration, temperature or electrostatic potential, then Laplace's equation is Fick's law of diffusion, Fourier's law of heat conduction or Ohm's law of electrical conduction, respectively. See Evans text for more.

Laplacians also appear in certain integral identities.

Theorem 14.5.7. Green's First Identity.

Let \(D\) denote a simply connected region with piecewise smooth boundary \(\partial D\text{.}\) Suppose that \(f\) is continuously differentiable and \(g\) is twice continuously differentiable on an open domain containing \(D\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \iint_{D}f\del^{2}g\,dA = \oint_{\partial D}f(\grad g)\dn - \iint_{D}\grad f\cdot \grad g\,dA\text{.} \end{equation*}

Proof.

Since the right hand side involves a flux integral, this suggests that Green's Theorem for Flux Integrals may prove useful. Applying this to \(\oint_{\partial D}f(\grad g)\dn\) gives

\begin{equation*} \oint_{\partial D}f(\grad g)\dn = \iint_{D}\div(f\grad g)\,dA\text{.} \end{equation*}

Now, recall that \(\div\) is a kind of derivative operator. For this reason, it also satisfies a version of the product rule:

\begin{equation*} \div(f\grad g) = \grad f\cdot \grad g + f \div(\grad g) = \grad f\cdot\grad g + f\del^{2}g\text{.} \end{equation*}

Plugging this into the double integral and rearranging proves the result.

Theorem 14.5.7 acts as a kind of “integration by parts” in higher dimensions.
