To see how the chain rule leads to an integration rule, suppose we have an expression \(F(g(x))\) where \(F,g\) are differentiable. Then the chain rule says that \(\dv{}{x}F(g(x)) = F'(g(x))g'(x).\) Now, write \(F' = f\) and make the substitution \(u = g(x)\text{.}\) Then we get

\begin{equation*} f(g(x))g'(x) = f(u)\dv{u}{x}. \end{equation*}

Now we can integrate both sides of this equation with respect to \(x\) to get

\begin{equation*} \int f(g(x))g'(x)\,dx = \int f(u)\dv{u}{x}\,dx = \int f(u)\,du. \end{equation*}

This method is known as \(u\)-substitution.
