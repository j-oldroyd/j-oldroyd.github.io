Example 11.2.13. Weight of a chain.

A still chain is fixed to two ends of a level divide. The tension of the chain at each fixed end can be represented by vectors pointing away from the chain. Call these tension forces \(\vv{T}_{1}\) and \(\vv{T}_{2}\text{.}\) Suppose we know that each force vector makes an angle of \(45^{\circ}\) with the ground on either side of the chain's fixed ends, and that the magnitude of each tension is 43 N. Then we can use vector addition to find the weight of the chain.

Let \(\vv{w}\) denote the weight of the chain considered as a vector (so that it's pointing down). Since the chain is still, its resultant 3  must be \(\vv{0}\text{.}\) So we can say that

\begin{equation*} \vv{T}_{1}+\vv{T}_{2} + \vv{w} = \vv{0} \end{equation*}

or in other words

\begin{equation*} \vv{w} = -\vv{T}_{1}-\vv{T}_{2}. \end{equation*}

What we need to find is \(\norm{\vv{w}}\text{,}\) which we can do without too much trouble if we can rewrite the tension vectors in component form. In fact, we have

\begin{align*} \vv{T}_{1} & = \dotprod{-\norm{\vv{T}_{1}}\cos45^{\circ},\norm{\vv{T}_{1}}\sin45^{\circ}} \\ \vv{T}_{2} & = \dotprod{\norm{\vv{T}_{2}}\cos45^{\circ},\norm{\vv{T}_{2}}\sin45^{\circ}} \end{align*}

So it follows that

\begin{align*} \norm{\vv{w}} & = \norm{-\vv{T}_{1}-\vv{T}_{2}} \\ & = \norm{\dotprod{0,-2\cdot43\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}}}\\ & = 43\sqrt{2} \end{align*}

Therefore the chain weighs \(43\sqrt{2}\)N.
