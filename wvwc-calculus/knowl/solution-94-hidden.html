First, note that we can rewrite DefinitionÂ 6.1.1 using sigma notation as

\begin{equation*} A = \lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^{n}f(x_{i})\Delta x. \end{equation*}

We know what \(f(x)\) is, so we just need to identify \(x_{i}\) and \(\Delta x\text{.}\) \(\Delta x\) isn't too bad here, it's just

\begin{equation*} \Delta x = \frac{3-1}{n} = \frac{2}{n}. \end{equation*}

The \(x_{i}\) are a little more complicated, but we'll still use right endpoints to find them. So we have

\begin{align*} x_{0} & = 1 \\ x_{1} & = 1 + \frac{2}{n} = \frac{n+2}{n} \\ x_{2} & = 1 + 2\frac{2}{n} = \frac{n+4}{n} \\ x_{3} & = 1 + 3\frac{2}{n} = \frac{n+6}{n} \\ & \vdots \\ x_{n} & = 3 \end{align*}

In general,

\begin{equation*} x_{i} = 1 + i\frac{2}{n} = \frac{n+2i}{n} \end{equation*}

for \(1\leq i\leq n\text{.}\)

Now we have everything we need to write our limit. Hence the area under \(\sin x\) from \(1\) to \(3\) is given by

\begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sin\left(\frac{n+2i}{n}\right)\frac{2}{n}. \end{equation*}
