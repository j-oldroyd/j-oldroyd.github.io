Proof

We can try to use power series to make sense of \(e^{ix}\text{.}\) Our idea is that since the power series for \(e^{x}\) is valid for all \(x\text{,}\) it should be valid for all \(ix\) as well, giving

\begin{align*} e^{ix} \amp 1 + (ix) + \frac{(ix)^{2}}{2!} + \frac{(ix)^{3}}{3!} + \frac{(ix)^{4}}{4!} + \cdots \\ \amp = \cos x + i\sin x \text{.} \end{align*}

In particular,

\begin{equation*} e^{i\pi} = -1\text{.} \end{equation*}
