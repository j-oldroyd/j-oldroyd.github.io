Example 10.3.3. Computing dot products.

Let \(\vv{x} = \dotprod{1,3,-1},\vv{y} = \dotprod{-3,1,0}\) and \(\vv{z} = \dotprod{1,1,1}\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \vv{x}\cdot\vv{z} = 1+3-1 = 3\quad\text{and}\quad\vv{x}\cdot\vv{y} = -3+3+0 = 0. \end{equation*}

It also doesn't matter what order we take the vectors in for the dot product: we get the same answer regardless. However, it does matter that we only use two vectors. The dot product takes two vectors and gives a scalar. In other words, it is impossible to take the dot product of more than two vectors at a time! So quantities such as \(\vv{x}\cdot\vv{y}\cdot\vv{z}\) are not meaningful.
