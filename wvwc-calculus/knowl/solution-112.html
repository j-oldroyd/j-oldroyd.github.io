Solution 7.1.3.1.

Since we need to integrate a product, and we can't easily do so with \(u\)-substitution or algebra, we'll try integration by parts. We could pick \(u = 3x^{2}\text{,}\) and this is certainly easy to differentiate so we can find \(du\text{.}\) However, doing so leaves us with \(dv = \ln x\text{,}\) which is tricky to integrate, but is easy to differentiate. So we'll try setting \(u = \ln x\) and \(dv = 3x^{2}\,dx\text{.}\) Then

\begin{align*} du & = \frac{1}{x}\,dx \\ v & = x^{3} \end{align*}

and so

\begin{align*} \int 3x^{2}\ln x\,dx & = uv - \int v\,du \\ & = x^{3}\ln x - \int \frac{x^{3}}{x}\,dx \\ & = x^{3}\ln x - \frac{1}{3}x^{3} + C. \end{align*}
