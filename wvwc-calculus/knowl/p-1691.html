To see how the Laplacian can arise, consider the following situation explained in Evans' Partial Differential Equations, \(2^{\text{nd}}\) edition. We have some density function \(f\text{;}\) this could be mass density, charge density, etc. Now we'll let \(\vb{F}\) denote the flux of \(f\text{.}\) It's often reasonable to assume that the flux is proportional to the negative of the gradient of \(f\text{:}\) \(\vb{F} = -a\grad f\) where \(a \gt 0\text{.}\) This means that the quantity flows from regions of higher concentration to regions of lower concentration. If \(u\) represents a quantity in equilibrium within some region \(D\text{,}\) then the net flux across \(D\) should be \(0\text{.}\) In terms of TheoremÂ 13.4.5, we have

\begin{equation*} \iint_{D}\div\vb{F}\,dA = \oint_{\partial D}\vb{F}\cdot\vb{n}\,ds = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
