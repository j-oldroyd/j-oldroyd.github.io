Place both vectors \(\vv{a}\) and \(\vv{b}\) at the origin, and then draw the vector \(\vv{a}-\vv{b}\) from the tip of \(\vv{b}\) to the tip of \(\vv{a}\text{,}\) like so:

Then \(\vv{a},\vv{b}\) and \(\vv{a}-\vv{b}\) form a triangle. We want to relate the dot product \(\vv{a}\cdot\vv{b}\) with the angle \(\theta\text{,}\) so we'll start by using the Law of Cosines to get an equation involving \(\theta\text{.}\) The Law of Cosines states that

\begin{equation} \norm{\vv{a}-\vv{b}}^{2} = \norm{\vv{a}}^{2}+\norm{\vv{b}}^{2} -2\norm{\vv{a}}\norm{\vv{b}}\cos\theta.\tag{11.3.1} \end{equation}

Here's where the dot product comes in. Each squared magnitude can be rewritten as a dot product using TheoremÂ 11.3.4, so in particular we have

\begin{equation*} \norm{\vv{a}-\vv{b}}^{2} = (\vv{a}-\vv{b})\cdot(\vv{a}-\vv{b}) = \norm{\vv{a}}^{2}-2(\vv{a}\cdot\vv{b}) + \norm{\vv{b}}^{2}. \end{equation*}

Plugging this into (11.3.1) gives us the following:

\begin{equation*} \norm{\vv{a}}^{2}-2(\vv{a}\cdot\vv{b})+\norm{\vv{b}}^{2} = \norm{\vv{a}}^{2}+\norm{\vv{b}}^{2} - 2\norm{\vv{a}}\norm{\vv{b}}\cos\theta \end{equation*}

Which finally simplifies down to

\begin{equation*} \vv{a}\cdot\vv{b} = \norm{\vv{a}}\norm{\vv{b}}\cos\theta. \end{equation*}
