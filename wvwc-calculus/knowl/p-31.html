Let's consider \(f(x) = x^2\) again and note that \((2, 4)\) is a point on the graph of \(y = x^{2}\text{.}\) We want to try to find the slope of the parabola at this point. Newton's approach was to consider the rise-over-run on the graph of \(y = x^{2}\) between \((2,4)\) and a point \((x_{0}, y_{0})\) on the graph that was “infinitely close” to \((2,4)\text{.}\) Newton handled this by introducing an infinitesimal quantity \(o\) and writing

\begin{equation*} x_{0} = 2 + o\quad\text{and}y_{0} = f(2 + o)\text{.} \end{equation*}

The corresponding rise-over-run is then

\begin{equation*} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{f(2 + o) - f(2)}{(2+o) - 2} = \frac{(2 +o)^2 - 4}{o}\text{,} \end{equation*}

which simplifies further to

\begin{equation*} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{4 + 4o + o^{2} - 4}{o} = 4 + o\text{.} \end{equation*}

At this point, Newton argued that \(o\text{,}\) being infinitesimal, contributed nothing to this quantity and simply ignored it. This leaves a value of \(4\) for the slope of \(y = x^{2}\) at \((2,4)\text{.}\) This answer is hand-wavy but does agree with our graphical intuition.
