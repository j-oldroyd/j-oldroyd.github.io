Section 14.4 Green's Theorem

Recall that \(\int_{C}\vb{F}\cdot\vb{T}\,ds = \int_{C} P\,dx + Q\,dy\) is the circulation of \(\vb{F}\) along \(C\text{.}\) Likewise, \(Q_{x} - P_{y}\) is a measure of how \(\vb{F}\) rotates counterclockwise. These two quantities are tied together by Green's Theorem.

Theorem 14.4.1. Green's Theorem.

Let \(C\) be a positively oriented, piecewise smooth, simple closed curve in the plane containing some region \(D\text{.}\) Let \(\vb{F} = \dotprod{P,Q}\) denote a vector field that is continuously differentiable on an open, simply connected domain containing \(C\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \oint_{C}\vb{F}\cdot\vb{T}\,ds = \iint_{D}\brackets{\pdv{Q}{x} - \pdv{P}{y}}\,dA\text{.} \end{equation*}

Example 14.4.2. Green's Theorem on a Triangle.

Compute \(\oint_{C}\vb{F}\dr\text{,}\) where \(\vb{F} = \dotprod{-3y, 3x}\) and \(C\) is the triangle with vertices \((0,0), (1,0)\) and \((0,2)\) traversed once counterclockwise.

Solution.

First, note that \(\vb{F}\) isn't conservative and so we can't just say the integral is \(0\text{.}\) Finding the line integral directly would require computing three separate line integrals, so we'll try Green's Theorem instead.

Let \(D\) denote the inside of the triangle. Then we have

\begin{equation*} \oint_{C}\vb{F}\dr = \iint_{D}\brackets{3 - (-3)}\,dA\text{,} \end{equation*}

which simplifies to \(6\text{.}\)

Example 14.4.3. Work with Green's Theorem.

Find the work done by \(\vb{F} = \dotprod{y + e^{-x^{3}}, 2x + \cos y^{2}}\) along the boundary of the region bounded by \(y = x^{2}\) and \(x = y^{2}\text{,}\) traversed clockwise exactly once.

Example 14.4.4. Circulation Along an Annulus.

Does \(\vb{F} = (1 - y^{3})\vb{i} + (x^{3} + e^{y^{2}})\vb{j}\) flow with the boundary of the annulus \(D\) bounded by \(x^{2} + y^{2} = 1\) and \(x^{2} + y^{2} = 9\text{?}\) Here, assume that the boundary of \(D\) is oriented in a way that is consistent with Green's Theorem.

Green's Theorem applies to circulation integrals, but there's also a version for flux integrals.

Theorem 14.4.5. Green's Theorem for Flux Integrals.

Let \(\vb{F}\text{,}\) \(C\) and \(D\) satisfy the same hypotheses as in Theorem 14.4.1. Then

\begin{equation*} \oint_{C}\vb{F}\cdot\vb{n}\,ds = \iint_{D}\brackets{\pdv{P}{x} + \pdv{Q}{y}}\,dA\text{.} \end{equation*}

The quantity \(P_{x} + Q_{y}\) is known as the divergence of \(\vb{F}\text{,}\) and measures how \(\vb{F}\) flows across a point.

Example 14.4.6. Flux Across a Square.

Does \(\vb{F} = \dotprod{y^{2} - x^{2}, x^{2} + y^{2}}\) tend to flow out of the unit square?
