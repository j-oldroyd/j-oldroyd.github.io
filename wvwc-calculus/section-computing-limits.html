Section 1.2 Computing Limits

¶

We've got a handle on how to estimate limits from Section 1.1, but the process is very tedious. It requires either graphing the function in question or laboriously entering values into a calculator. So our first order of business now that we have the concept of a limit is to find an easier way to calculate it. This will be a running theme throughout the course.

Subsection 1.2.1 The Limit Laws

¶

In many cases of interest, we can use knowledge of simpler limits to obtain more complicated limits. We do this via the Limit Laws. Before we get to them, we'll state two very simple (and hopefully very believable) limits.

Proposition 1.2.1. Simple Limits.

For any value of \(a\text{,}\) the following limits hold:

\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to a}c = c \end{equation*}

if \(c\) is a constant and

\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to a}x = a. \end{equation*}

Theorem 1.2.2 gives us the ability to compute a wide variety of limits.

Example 1.2.4. Limit of a rational function.

Let

\begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{3 - x^{5} + 5x}{2x-\sqrt[4]{x}}. \end{equation*}

Evaluate \(\lim_{x\to 16}f(x)\text{.}\)

Solution

We can evaluate \(\lim_{x\to 16}f(x)\) by making use of the appropriate Limit Laws and Proposition 1.2.1:

\begin{align*} \lim_{x\to 16}\frac{3 - x^{5} + 5x}{2x-\sqrt[4]{x}} & = \frac{\lim_{x\to 16}(3 - x^{5} + 5x)}{\lim_{x\to 16}(2x-\sqrt[4]{x})} & \text{by Limit Law 4}\\ & = \frac{\lim_{x\to 16}3 - \lim_{x\to 16}x^{5} + \lim_{x\to 16}5x}{\lim_{x\to 16}2x-\lim_{x\to 16}\sqrt[4]{x}} & \text{by Limit Law 1.} \\ & = \frac{3 - [\lim_{x\to 16}x]^{5} + 5\lim_{x\to 16}x}{2\lim_{x\to 16}x-\sqrt[4]{\lim_{x\to 16}x}} & \text{by Limit Laws 2, 5, and 6.} \\ & = \frac{3-16^{5}+80}{30} & \end{align*}

In particular, Limit Laws 1-5 give us the following: if \(f(x)\) is a polynomial or rational function, then \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x) = f(a)\) as long as \(a\) is in the domain of \(f(x)\text{.}\) If \(a\) is not in the domain, trickery may be required.

Example 1.2.5. Trickery.

Evaluate

\begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to1}\frac{\sqrt{x}-1}{x-1}. \end{equation*}
Solution

First, note that we can't use the Limit Laws right away since the denominator is \(0\) at \(x=1\text{.}\) What we need to do is use algebra to simplify the expression inside the limit:

\begin{align*} \frac{\sqrt{x}-1}{x-1} & = \frac{\sqrt{x}-1}{x-1}\frac{\sqrt{x}+1}{\sqrt{x}+1} \\ & = \frac{x - 1}{(x-1)(\sqrt{x}+1)} \\ & = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}+1}. \end{align*}

Now we can use the Limit Laws to find the limit as \(x\) approaches \(1\text{,}\) since we no longer have a divide-by-zero problem in the denominator:

\begin{align*} \lim_{x\to1}\frac{\sqrt{x}-1}{x-1} & = \lim_{x\to1}\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}+1} \\ & = \frac{1}{2}. \end{align*}
