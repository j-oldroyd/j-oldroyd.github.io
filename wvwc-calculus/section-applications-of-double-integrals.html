Section 13.4 Applications of Double Integrals

Subsection 13.4.1 Mass

Consider a thin plate in the \(xy\)-plane, say the region \(R\text{.}\) If the density of the plate \(\rho(x,y)\) at \((x,y)\) is constant, say \(\rho(x,y) = C\text{,}\) then the mass of the plate is just the density times the area. On the other hand, if the density is varies then this becomes more complicated, and we must use double integrals. In particular, the mass of a plate contained in the region \(R\) in the \(xy\)-plane with density \(\rho(x,y)\) is given by

\begin{equation*} \iint_{R}\rho(x,y)\,dA \end{equation*}

Example 13.4.1. Mass of a triangular plate.

Find the mass of the plate contained in the triangular region \(D\) bounded by lines \(y=x, y = 6\) and \(2x+y = 6\text{,}\) given that the density is \(\rho(x,y) = x^{2}\text{.}\)

Solution.

The mass is

\begin{align*} \iint_{D}x^{2}\,A & = \int_{2}^{6}\int_{\frac{6-y}{2}}^{y}x^{2}\,dx\,dy\\ & = \frac{1}{3}\int_{2}^{6}\left[\frac{y^{3} - (6-y)^{3}}{8}\right]\,dy \end{align*}

The moments of the mass contained in \(D\) and with density \(\rho(x,y)\) are defined as follows:

\begin{align*} M_{x} & = \iint_{D}y\rho(x,y)\,dA \\ M_{y} & = \iint_{D}x\rho(x,y)\,dA \end{align*}

If we let \(m\) denote the total mass, then we also define the center of mass (or centroid) to be the point \((\bar{x},\bar{y})\text{,}\) where

\begin{align*} \bar{x} & = \frac{M_{y}}{m} \\ \bar{y} & = \frac{M_{x}}{m} \end{align*}

Example 13.4.2. Center of mass of an annulus.

Find the center of mass of the plate \(D\) outside of the circle \(x^{2}+y^{2} = 1\) and inside the circle \(x^{2} + y^{2} = 4\text{,}\) with density \(\rho(x,y) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x^{2} + y^{2}}}\text{.}\)

Solution.

The mass is given by

\begin{align*} \iint_{D} \frac{1}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}}\,dA & = \int_{0}^{2\pi}\int_{1}^{2}\frac{r}{r}\,dr\,d\theta \\ & = 2\pi \end{align*}

The moments are

\begin{align*} M_{x} & = \iint_{D}\frac{y}{\sqrt{x^{2} + y^{2}}}\,dA \\ & = \int_{0}^{2\pi}\int_{1}^{2} \frac{r\sin\theta}{r}r\,dr\,d\theta \\ & = 0 \end{align*}

and likewise \(M_{y} = 0\text{.}\) So the center of mass is \((0,0)\text{.}\)
