The process outlined above and in Example 2.27 is known as diagonalization. This is only possible when \(A\) has an eigenbasis to work with, but can lead to vastly more efficient computations involving \(A\) by making use of the formula

\begin{equation*} A^n = PD^nP^{-1} \end{equation*}

since raising diagonal matrices to a power is much simpler than raising general matrices to a power. Finding \(A^{100}\bb\) in Example 2.27 was equivalent to the following computations:

\begin{align*} A^{100}\bb & = PD^{100}P^{-1}\bb \\ & = \mqty[1 & 3 \\ 1 & 4]\mqty[1^{100} & 0 \\ 0 & 2^{100}]\mqty[4 & -3 \\ -1 & 1]\mqty[-2 \\ 10] \\ & = \mqty[1 & 3 \\ 1 & 4]\mqty[1 & 0 \\ 0 & 2^{100}]\mqty[-38 \\ 12] \\ & = \mqty[1 & 3 \\ 1 & 4]\mqty[-38 \\ 12(2^{100})] \\ & = \mqty[-38 + 36(2^{100}) \\ -38 + 48(2^{100})] \text{.} \end{align*}
