TheoremÂ 4.5.1 leads to several other integral formulas that can be useful in higher dimensions. For our first example, we'll consider what happens if \(\FF\) is conservative. If this is the case, then there exists a potential function \(f\) for \(\FF\text{:}\) \(\del{f} = \FF\text{.}\) Then
\begin{align*} \div\FF \amp = \div\del{f} = \Delta f\\ \FF\cdot\dd{\SS} \amp = \del{f}\cdot\nn\dd{S} \end{align*}
where \(\Delta f = f_{xx} + f_{yy}\) is the Laplacian of \(f\) and \(\del{f}\cdot\nn\) is the normal derivative of \(f\text{,}\) often denoted by \(\pdv{f}{n}\text{.}\)
