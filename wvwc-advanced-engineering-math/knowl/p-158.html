Since \(A\) is triangular, its eigenvalues are \(0\) and \(1\text{.}\) We also know at this point that \(A\) can't be defective. In particular, we can form a basis of \(\RR^2\) entirely from eigenvectors of \(A\text{.}\) To actually find the eigenvectors, we need to find the corresponding eigenspaces \(E_{0} = \null(A-0I) = \null A\) and \(E_{1} = \null(A-I)\text{.}\)
