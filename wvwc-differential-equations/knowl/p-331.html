Let \(A\) be an \(n\times n\) matrix and suppose that \(\vec{v}\) is an eigenvector with corresponding eigenvalue \(\lambda\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} A\vec{v} = \lambda\vec{v}. \end{equation*}

We can rearrange this to get

\begin{equation*} A\vec{v}-\lambda\vec{v} = (A-\lambda I)\vec{v} = \vec{0} \end{equation*}

where \(I\) is the identity matrix. Since \(\vec{v}\neq\vec{0}\) (since it's an eigenvector!), linear algebra tells us that \(\det(A-\lambda I) = 0\text{.}\) This gives us the following theorem.
