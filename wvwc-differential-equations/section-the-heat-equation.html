Section 8.4 The Heat Equation

¶

The last equation we will look at is the heat equation, which models the temperature distribution of a thin bar of uniform density and constant cross-section placed along the \(x\)-axis. We also assume that the bar is perfectly insulated on its surface, so that heat flows along the bar in the \(x\)-direction only. With these assumptions, the temperature \(u(x,t)\) of the bar at position \(x\) and time \(t\) satisfies the PDE

\begin{align*} \pdv{u}{t} \amp = c^{2}\pdv[2]{u}{x} \text{.} \end{align*}

This is called the one-dimensional heat equation.

Subsection 8.4.1 Bar with ends fixed at \(0\)

¶

We will start by solving the heat equation for the case where the bar has ends which are fixed at temperature \(0\text{.}\) If we're given an initial temperature distribution \(f(x)\text{,}\) then \(u(x,t)\) is the solution of the boundary value problem

\begin{align} u_{t} \amp = c^{2}u_{xx} \label{equation-one-dimensional-heat-equation}\tag{8.4.1}\\ u(0,t) = u(L,t) \amp = 0 \label{heat-equation-boundary-conditions-fixed-temperature}\tag{8.4.2}\\ u(x,0) \amp = f(x) \text{.}\label{heat-equation-initial-condition}\tag{8.4.3} \end{align}

We can solve this boundary value problem using separation of variables, much as we did in Section 8.2. So to start, we assume that \(u(x,t) = X(x)T(t)\text{.}\) If we plug this into the heat equation (8.4.1), then we get

\begin{equation} \frac{T'}{c^{2}T} = \frac{X''}{X} = k.\label{equation-odes-from-heat-equation}\tag{8.4.4} \end{equation}

Now we have three separate cases to consider for \(k\text{:}\) \(k\gt0, k=0\) or \(k\lt0\text{.}\) Just as with the wave equation, the only case that doesn't lead to trivial solutions is \(k=-\mu^{2}\lt0\text{.}\) In this case (8.4.4) leads to the two ODEs given by

\begin{align*} X''+\mu^{2}X \amp = 0 \\ T'+c^{2}\mu^{2}T \amp = 0 \text{.} \end{align*}

The boundary conditions in (8.4.2) force \(X(0) = X(L) = 0\text{,}\) and the only nontrivial solutions of \(X''+\mu^{2}X = 0\) occur when \(\mu = \frac{n\pi}{L}\text{.}\) So we get the solutions \(X = X_{n} = \sin\frac{n\pi}{L}x\text{,}\) just as with the wave equation.

For the second ODE, we readily solve it to obtain \(T = T_{n} = b_{n}e^{-\lambda_{n}^{2}t}\) where \(\lambda_{n} = c\frac{n\pi}{L}\) as before. So every function

\begin{equation*} u_{n}(x,t) = X_{n}T_{n} = b_{n}\sin\frac{n\pi}{L}xe^{-\lambda_{n}^{2}t} \end{equation*}

is a solution of (8.4.1) that satisfies the boundary equations (8.4.2). In order to satisfy the arbitrary initial condition \(f(x)\text{,}\) we take an infinite sum of the functions \(u_{n}(x,t)\) to get

\begin{equation*} u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}b_{n}\sin\frac{n\pi}{L}x e^{-\lambda^{2}_{n}t}. \end{equation*}

Finally, if we plug in \(t=0\) and use the initial condition \(u(x,0) = f(x)\text{,}\) we get \(f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}b_{n}\sin\frac{n\pi}{L}x\text{.}\) This is just the Fourier series of the odd extension of \(f(x)\text{,}\) which lets us find \(b_{n}\text{.}\) We summarize all of this in the following theorem.

Theorem 8.4.1. Solution of the Heat Equation with Fixed Temperature.

The solution of the heat equation (8.4.1) satisfying the boundary conditions (8.4.2) and initial condition (8.4.3) is given by

\begin{equation} u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}b_{n}\sin\frac{n\pi}{L}x e^{-\lambda^{2}_{n}t}\label{heat-equation-fixed-ends-solution}\tag{8.4.5} \end{equation}

where

\begin{align} b_{n} \amp = \frac{2}{L}\int_{0}^{L}f(x)\sin\frac{n\pi}{L}x\,dx \label{heat-equation-fixed-ends-solution-coeff}\tag{8.4.6}\\ \lambda_{n} \amp = c\frac{n\pi}{L}. \label{heat-equation-fixed-ends-solution-exponent}\tag{8.4.7} \end{align}
Example 8.4.2. Sinusoidal initial temperature.

Consider a thin metal bar of length \(\pi\) placed on the \(x\)-axis, with one end at \(x=0\) and the other at \(x=\pi\text{.}\) Assuming that \(c=1\) and that the initial temperature is \(f(x) = 30\sin x\) for \(0\leq x\leq\pi\text{,}\) find the temperature distribution using Theorem 8.4.1.

Solution

The temperature is the function \(u(x,t)\) given by

\begin{equation*} u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}b_{n}\sin(n x)e^{-n^{2}t}, \end{equation*}

where \(b_{n}\) is the \(n^{\th}\) coefficient of the Fourier series of the odd extension of \(f(x)\text{.}\) The odd extension of \(f(x)\) is \(30\sin x\text{.}\) Furthermore, the Fourier series of \(30\sin x\) is clearly just \(30\sin x\text{.}\)

So in other words,

\begin{equation*} b_{n} = \begin{cases} 30 \amp n=1 \\ 0 \amp otherwise.\end{cases} \end{equation*}

Hence

\begin{equation*} u(x,t) = 30e^{-t}\sin x. \end{equation*}
