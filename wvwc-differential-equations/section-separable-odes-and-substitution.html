Section 1.3 Separable ODEs and Substitution

This section corresponds to Section 1.3 from the text.

Subsection 1.3.1 Separable ODEs

The simplest ODEs to solve are the first-order ODEs of the form \(\dv{y}{x} = f(x)\text{.}\) The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus guarantees that the solution \(y\) is given by \(y = \int f(x)\,dx\text{.}\)

Rather, the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus guarantees that the solution will be \(y=\int f(x)\,dx\) as long as \(f\) is a continuous function.

Another type of ODE that is relatively straightforward to solve is the separable ODE, which is a first-order ODE that can be written in the form

\begin{equation*} \dv{y}{x} = f(x)g(y). \end{equation*}

These ODEs can be solved by integration as well, but only after some rearranging.

Example 1.3.1. Solving a separable ODE.

Solve the IVP given by \(y' = x+4xy^{2}, y(0)=1\text{.}\)

Solution

The first step to solving this IVP is to solve the ODE \(y' = x+4xy^{2}\text{.}\) It may not look like it at first, but this ODE is separable since we can rewrite it as \(y' = x(1+4y^{2})\text{.}\) To solve this ODE, we need to move the \(y\) terms to the left hand side of the equation and the \(x\) terms to the right hand side. We'll abuse notation a little bit to do so by rewriting \(y' = \dv{y}{x}\) and treating \(\dv{y}{x}\) as a fraction, but it won't get us into too much trouble here:

\begin{align*} \dv{y}{x} = x(1+4y^{2}) &\Rightarrow \frac{dy}{1+4y^{2}} = x\,dx \\ &\Rightarrow \int\frac{dy}{1+4y^{2}} = \int x\,dx \\ &\Rightarrow \frac{1}{2}\tan^{-1}(2y) = \frac{1}{2}x^{2}+C \\ &\Rightarrow \tan^{-1}(2y) = x^{2}+C_{1} \end{align*}

At this step we can either leave the solution as is (in implicit form) or solve for \(y\) to get an explicit form. We'll leave this in implicit form and then plug in the initial condition to get

\begin{equation*} \tan^{-1}(2) = C_{1}. \end{equation*}

So the implicit solution of this IVP is given by

\begin{equation*} \tan^{-1}(2y) = x^{2}+\tan^{-1}(2). \end{equation*}
Example 1.3.2. Newton's Law of Cooling again.

A metal plate is removed from an oven and placed in a room. The temperature of the plate is \(100^{\circ}\) Celsius and the temperature of the room is fixed at \(15^{\circ}\) Celsius. After \(20\) minutes the temperature of the plate drops to \(90^{\circ}\) Celsius. How hot is the plate after five hours?

Solution

Let \(T(t)\) denote the temperature of the plate \(t\) minutes after being removed from the oven and let \(A(t)\) denote the temperature of the room \(t\) minutes after the plate is removed from the oven. Then \(T(0) = 100\) and Newton's Law of Cooling says that

\begin{equation*} \dv{T}{t} = k(T-15). \end{equation*}

To answer this question we need to find \(T\text{,}\) and although we don't know \(k\) at the moment we can still make some progress just by remembering that it's a constant. This ODE is separable, so we'll separate variables and integrate both sides to get

\begin{equation*} \ln(T-15) = kt+C \end{equation*}

which simplifies to \(T-15 = C_{1}e^{kt}\) or just \(T = 15+C_{1}e^{kt}\text{.}\)

Now we need to find \(C_{1}\) and \(k\text{.}\) To find \(C_{1}\text{,}\) we just use the initial condition to get \(C_{1} = 85\text{.}\) The only piece of information that we have left to find \(k\) is the fact that the temperature of the plate drops to \(90\) after \(20\) minutes. In other words, \(T(20) = 90\text{.}\) Therefore

\begin{equation*} 90 = 15+85e^{20k} \end{equation*}

which becomes

\begin{equation*} \ln\frac{75}{85} = 20k\text{.} \end{equation*}

Therefore \(k = \frac{1}{20}\ln\frac{15}{17}\approx-.006.\)

So, finally, \(T(t) = 15+85e^{-.006t}\) and the temperature after five hours is \(T(300)\approx28\text{.}\)

Subsection 1.3.2 Substitution Methods

At this point we can only solve a couple types of differential equations. An ODE that isn't of the form \(\dv{y}{x} = f(x)\) or separable may prove troublesome. However, there are certain cases where we can rewrite an ODE into one of these forms by using the right substitution.

Example 1.3.3. Substitution to solve an ODE.

Find the general solution of \(y' = (x+y+3)^{2}\text{.}\)

Solution

This ODE is not separable and we can't just integrate it (since the right hand side depends on the dependent variable). However, the form of the right hand side suggests a substitution: \(u = x+y+3\text{.}\) This would simplify things quite a bit, leaving us with \(y'=u^{2}\text{.}\) The only problem with this is that \(y'\) depends on \(x\text{,}\) not \(u\text{.}\) We must rewrite \(y'\) in terms of the new variable \(u\text{,}\) which isn't too bad. Since \(u = x+y+3\text{,}\) we get \(y' = u'-1\text{.}\) Therefore the ODE becomes

\begin{equation*} u'-1 = u^{2}\text{ or just }\dv{u}{x} = 1+u^{2}. \end{equation*}

This new ODE is separable, and so we separate variables and integrate to get \(\tan^{-1}u = x+C\text{.}\) If we don't care about finding an explicit solution, then we can just replace \(u\) to get the equation back in terms of \(y\text{.}\) So our (implicit) general solution is \(\tan^{-1}(x+y+3) = x+C\text{.}\)

Example 1.3.4. A less obvious substitution.

Find an explicit solution of \(xy'=x+y\text{.}\)

Solution

It's tough to see what to do right away, so we'll try simplifying the ODE first. In particular, we'll solve for \(y'\) to get

\begin{equation*} y' = 1+\frac{y}{x}. \end{equation*}

If we stare at this for a while, we might convince ourselves that the right hand side really just depends on \(\frac{y}{x}\text{,}\) so we'll try replacing that with \(u\text{.}\) Then the ODE becomes \(y'=1+u\text{.}\)

Once again, this is much simpler but we need to rewrite \(y'\) in terms of \(u\text{.}\) Since \(u=\frac{y}{x}\text{,}\) this means that \(y=ux\) and so \(y' = u+u'x\text{.}\) Then the ODE becomes

\begin{equation*} u+x\dv{u}{x} = 1+u\text{ or just } x\dv{u}{x} = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}

This new ODE can be rearranged to get \(\dv{u}{x} = \frac{1}{x}\text{,}\) and so \(u = \ln|x|+C\text{.}\) Getting back in terms of \(y\text{,}\) we have \(\frac{y}{x} = \ln|x|+C\) or just \(y = x\ln|x|+Cx\text{.}\)
