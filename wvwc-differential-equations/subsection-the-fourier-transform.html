Section 5.4 The Fourier Transform

¶

If \(f(x)\) is a periodic function with period \(p=2L\text{,}\) then we know how to find its Fourier series, both real and complex. But what do we do if our function \(f(x)\) is not periodic? Can we still get a similar representation?

Let \(f(x)\) be some piecewise-differentiable function, not necessarily periodic. Then we can't find it's Fourier series. However, we can truncate the graph of \(f(x)\text{,}\) and replace it with a periodic function that is equal to \(f(x)\) on some interval \((-L,L)\text{.}\) Then we can find the Fourier series of this function, which by Theorem Theorem 5.3.1 is given by \(\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}c_{k}e^{i\frac{k\pi}{L}x}\) where

\begin{equation*} c_{k} = \frac{1}{2L}\int_{-L}^{L}f(x)e^{-i\frac{k\pi}{L}x}\,dx. \end{equation*}

So we can write

\begin{equation*} f(x) = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}\left(\frac{1}{2L}\int_{-L}^{L}f(x)e^{-i\frac{k\pi}{L}x}\,dx\right)e^{i\frac{k\pi}{L}x} \end{equation*}

wherever \(f(x)\) is continuous on \(-L<x<x\text{.}\) The idea now is that the larger that \(T\) gets, this expression can be used to represent \(f(x)\) for more and more values of \(x\text{.}\) So we want to see what happens to this as \(T\to\infty\text{.}\) First, we'll clean this up a little bit by writing \(w_{k} = \frac{k\pi}{L}\) and \(\Delta w = w_{k+1} - w_{k}\text{,}\) so that \(\frac{1}{2L} = \frac{\Delta w}{2\pi}\text{.}\) Then if \(x\) is in \((-L,L)\) and \(f\) is continuous at \(x\text{,}\) then we can say

\begin{align*} f(x) & = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}\left(\frac{1}{2L}\int_{-L}^{L}f(x)e^{-i\frac{k\pi}{L}x}\,dx\right)e^{i\frac{k\pi}{L}x} \\ & = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty}\left(\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-L}^{L}f(x)e^{-iw_{k}x}\,dx\right)e^{iw_{k}x}\Delta w. \end{align*}

As awful as this looks, we can relate this to a Riemann sum! As \(L\to\infty,\) \(\Delta w\to0\text{,}\) we can replace \(w_{k}\) with the new variable \(\omega\) and this expression becomes

\begin{equation*} f(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\left(\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(x)e^{-i\omega x}\,dx\right)e^{i\omega x}\,d\omega. \end{equation*}

This leads to the definition of the Fourier transform. But first we need another definition.

Definition 5.4.1 Absolutely Integrable Functions

Let \(f(x)\) be a piecewise continuous function. Then \(f(x)\) is absolutely integrable if \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|f(x)|\,dx<\infty.\)

Definition 5.4.2 The Fourier Transform

Let \(f(x)\) be an absolutely integrable piecewise continuous function. The Fourier transform of \(f(x)\) is the function \(\hat{f}(\omega)\) defined by

\begin{equation*} \hat{f}(\omega) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(x)e^{-i\omega x}\,dx. \end{equation*}

We often write \(\mathcal{F}(f)\) to denote the Fourier transform as well.

Example 5.4.3 Fourier transform of a piecewise exponential

Let \(f(x) = e^{-2x}\) for \(x>-5\) and \(0\) otherwise. Then the Fourier transform of \(f(x)\) is

\begin{align*} \hat{f}(\omega) & = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(x)e^{-i\omega x}\,dx \\ & = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-5}^{\infty}e^{-2x}e^{-i\omega x}\,dx \\ & = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-5}^{\infty}e^{-(2+i\omega)x}\,dx \\ & = -\frac{1}{(2+i\omega)\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-(2+i\omega)x}\Big]_{-5}^{\infty}\\ & = \frac{e^{(2+i\omega)5}}{(2+i\omega)\sqrt{2\pi}}. \end{align*}

As with the Laplace transform, the Fourier transform of a function is said to be in the frequency domain. In fact, the magnitude of \(\hat{f}(\omega)\) represents the frequency content of the function \(f(x)\) (thought of as a signal) at the frequency \(\omega\text{.}\) It's also a quick jump to get the inverse Fourier transform.

Definition 5.4.4 The Inverse Fourier Transform

The inverse Fourier transform of \(\hat{f}(\omega)\) is

\begin{equation*} \mathcal{F}^{-1}(\hat{f}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\hat{f}(\omega)e^{i\omega x}\,d\omega. \end{equation*}
Theorem 5.4.5 Fourier Inversion Theorem

Let \(f(x)\) be an absolutely integrable, piecewise differentiable function. Then \(f(x) = \mathcal{F}^{-1}(\hat{f})\) wherever \(f(x)\) is continuous.

Example 5.4.6 Inverse Fourier transform of a step function

Define \(\hat{f}(\omega)\) by

\begin{equation*} \hat{f}(\omega) = \begin{cases} 1 & -1 < x < 1 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise.}\end{cases} \end{equation*}

Then we can find the inverse transform using Definition 5.4.4:

\begin{align*} \mathcal{F}^{-1}\{\hat{f}\} & = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\hat{f}(\omega)e^{i\omega x}\,d\omega \\ & = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-1}^{1}e^{i\omega x}\,d\omega \\ & = \frac{1}{2\pi}\frac{e^{i\omega x}}{ix}\Big]_{-1}^{1} \\ & = \frac{1}{2\pi}\frac{e^{ix}-e^{-ix}}{ix} \\ & = \frac{1}{\pi}\frac{\sin x}{x}. \end{align*}

The Fourier and inverse Fourier transforms are also linear like the Laplace transform: if \(a,b\) are constants and \(f,g\) are functions, then

\begin{equation*} \mathcal{F}\{af+bg\} = a\hat{f}+b\hat{g} \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} \mathcal{F}^{-1}(a\hat{f}+b\hat{g}) = af + bg. \end{equation*}

The Fourier transform also works well with derivatives.

Theorem 5.4.7 Fourier Transforms and Derivatives

Let \(f(x)\) be differentiable with derivative \(f'(x)\text{.}\) Suppose that both \(f(x)\) and \(f'(x)\) are absolutely integrable. Then

\begin{equation*} \mathcal{F}(f') = i\omega\hat{f}(\omega). \end{equation*}

Fourier transforms also behave well with another type of convolution.

Theorem 5.4.8 Convolution Theorem

Suppose that \(f(x),g(x)\) are piecewise continuous, bounded and absolutely integrable. Define \(f\ast g\) by

\begin{equation*} f\ast g = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(\tau)g(x-\tau)\,d\tau. \end{equation*}

Then \(\mathcal{F}(f\ast g) = \hat{f}\hat{g}.\)
