    Section 5.1 Power Series Method

    In calculus, it‚Äôs important to know how to differentiate and integrate functions. For some functions (say, \(x-1,x^{2},3-x^{5}\)) it can be very straightforward, but for others (such as \(e^{-x^{2}}\)) it can be impossible.
    At least, it can be impossible to integrate certain functions in terms of more ``everyday‚Äô‚Äô functions that we‚Äôre used to.
    So we‚Äôd like to see if there‚Äôs a way to write complicated functions \(f(x)\) in terms of simpler functions \(1,x,x^{2},\dots\text{.}\) To see how, let \(f(x)\) be a function. Our goal is to write it in the form
    \begin{equation} f(x) = c_{0}+c_{1}x+c_{2}x^{2}+c_{3}x^{3}+\dots = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}c_{k}x^{k}\tag{8} \end{equation}
    where the \(c_{k}\) are constants.

    Definition 5.1.1. Power Series.

    A power series is a series (that is, an infinite sum) of the form \(\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}c_{k}x^{k}\text{.}\) Since a power series is in general an infinite sum, it may not make sense for all values of \(x\text{.}\) The power series is said to converge on some interval \(I\) if the sum exists for each \(x\) in \(I\text{.}\)
    A power series doesn‚Äôt have to start at \(k=0\text{,}\) but it may not contain any negative powers of \(x\).
    The question now is, how do we find the right values of the coefficients \(c_{k}\) to make (8) true? If we look at the equation, we can solve for \(c_{0}\) very easily: just set \(x=0\) to make all other terms disappear:
    \begin{equation*} f(0) = c_{0} + c_{1}\cdot0+\dots = c_{0}. \end{equation*}
    To solve for \(c_{1}\) by plugging in \(x=0\text{,}\) we need to get rid of the power of \(x\) attached to it. We can do this by taking the derivative of \(f(x)\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} f'(x) &= c_{1} + 2c_{2}x + 3c_{3}x^{2} + \dots\\ f'(0) &= c_{1}\text{.} \end{align*}
    The same trick works for \(c_{2}\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} f''(x) &= 2\cdot1c_{2} + 3\cdot2c_{3}x+\dots\\ f''(0) &= 2\cdot1c_{2} \end{align*}
    so \(c_{2} = \frac{f''(0)}{2\cdot1}\text{.}\) Let‚Äôs try this one more time to get \(c_{3}\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} f^{(3)}(x) &= 3\cdot2\cdot1c_{3} + \dots\\ f^{(3)}(0) &= 3\cdot2\cdot1c_{3} \end{align*}
    and so \(c_{3} = \frac{f^{(3)}(0)}{3\cdot2\cdot1}\text{.}\)
    In general, to get the coefficient \(c_{k}\) of \(x^{k}\) in the power series of \(f(x)\text{,}\) we have the following equation:
    \begin{equation} c_{k} = \frac{f^{(k)}(0)}{k\cdot(k-1)\cdot\dots\cdot2\cdot1} = \frac{f^{(k)}(0)}{k!}.\tag{9} \end{equation}

    Example 5.1.2.

    Find a power series for \(e^{x}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Any power series for \(f(x) = e^{x}\) looks like \(\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}c_{k}x^{k}\text{,}\) where
    \begin{equation*} c_{k} = \frac{f^{(k)}(0)}{k!}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(e^{x}\) is its own derivative, \(f^{(k)}(x) = e^{x}\) for all choices of \(k\text{.}\) So
    \begin{equation*} c_{k} = \frac{e^{0}}{k!} = \frac{1}{k!} \end{equation*}
    and the power series for \(e^{x}\) is
    \begin{equation*} 1+x+\frac{x^{2}}{2}+\frac{x^{3}}{6} + \dots = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{x^{k}}{k!}. \end{equation*}
    It turns out the \(f(x) = e^{x}\) equals its power series for all values of \(x\text{.}\)
    The above power series was written in terms of powers of \(x\text{,}\) but this doesn‚Äôt have to be the case. We can also write power series in terms of powers of \(x-a\text{,}\) where \(a\) is some constant. A power series of the form
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}c_{k}(x-a)^{k} \end{equation*}
    is said to be centered at \(a\). For such a series, the formula for the \(c_{k}\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} c_{k} = \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}. \end{equation*}

    Example 5.1.3.

    Find the power series for \(g(t) = \sin t\) centered at \(a = \frac{\pi}{2}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    A power series centered at \(a = \frac{\pi}{2}\) will look like
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}c_{k}\parens{t-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{k} \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{equation*} c_{k} = \frac{g^{(k)}(\frac{\pi}{2})}{k!}. \end{equation*}
    To find these values, we need to compute the derivatives of \(g(t)\) and evaluate them at \(\frac{\pi}{2}\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} g^{(0)}(t) &= \sin t &\Rightarrow g^{(0)}(\frac{\pi}{2}) = 1\\ g'(t) &= \cos t &\Rightarrow g'(\frac{\pi}{2}) = 0\\ g''(t) &= -\sin t &\Rightarrow g''(\frac{\pi}{2}) = -1\\ g^{(3)}(t) &= -\cos t &\Rightarrow g^{(3)}(\frac{\pi}{2}) = 0\text{.} \end{align*}
    So the power series centered at \(\frac{\pi}{2}\) is
    \begin{equation*} 1 - \frac{1}{2!}(t-\frac{\pi}{2})^{2} + \frac{1}{4!}(t-\frac{\pi}{2})^{4}+\dots = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}(-1)^{k}\frac{(t-\frac{\pi}{2})^{2k}}{(2k)!}. \end{equation*}
    Just as with \(e^{x}\text{,}\) \(\sin t\) is equal to its power series everywhere.
    More functions in terms of power series:
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{1-x} &= \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}x^{n} & &= 1 + x + x^{2} + x^{3} + \cdots\\ e^{x} &= \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{x^{n}}{n!} & &= 1 + x + \frac{x^{2}}{2!} + \frac{x^{3}}{3!} + \cdots\\ \sin x &= \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(-1)^{n}\frac{x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!} & &= x - \frac{x^{3}}{3!} + \frac{x^{5}}{5!} - \cdots\\ \cos x &= \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(-1)^{n}\frac{x^{2n}}{(2n)!} & &= 1 - \frac{x^{2}}{2!} + \frac{x^{4}}{4!} - \cdots \end{align*}
    Viewing a function as a power series can be extremely beneficial; if you have a power series expression for some function, it is extremely easy to do calculus with it.

    Example 5.1.4.

    Find \(\displaystyle\int_{0}^{1}e^{x^{2}}\dd{x}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    We can‚Äôt integrate \(e^{x^{2}}\) using elementary functions
    We can use a function known as the error function.
    but this is straightforward to integrate using power series:
    \begin{align*} \int_{0}^{1}e^{x^{2}}\dd{x} &= \int_{0}^{1}\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{x^{2n}}{n!}\dd{x}\\ &= \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\int_{0}^{1}\frac{x^{2n}}{n!}\dd{x}\\ &= \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{1}{(2n+1)n!}\text{.} \end{align*}
    The following theorem can be used to determine when a power series converges (that is, when the series makes sense).

    Theorem 5.1.5.

    Given the power series \(\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}c_{n}x^{n}\text{,}\) define the number \(\rho\) by the limit
    \begin{equation*} \rho = \limit{n}{\infty}\left|\frac{c_{n}}{c_{n+1}}\right|. \end{equation*}
    Suppose the limit exists or is infinite. Then \(\rho\) is the radius of converengence of the series: if \(\rho\neq0\) then the series converges for \(|x|\lt\rho\) and diverges for \(|x|\gt\rho\text{.}\) If \(\rho=0\) then the series converges only at \(x=0\text{.}\)
    Our main use of power series will be in solving differential equations.

    Example 5.1.6.

    Solve the ODE given by
    \begin{equation*} \dv{y}{x} = 4y. \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    We could easily solve this using Chapter 1 methods, but we‚Äôll use power series to practice. To start, we assume that the solution \(y\) can be written as a power series:
    \begin{equation*} y = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}c_{n}x^{n}. \end{equation*}
    The next step is to plug this into the ODE. Since
    \begin{align*} \dv{y}{x} &= \dv{}{x}\parens{\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}c_{n}x^{n}}\\ &= \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}nc_{n}x^{n-1} \end{align*}
    we get the equation
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}nc_{n}x^{n-1} = 4\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}c_{n}x^{n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We need to find the values of the coefficients \(c_{n}\text{;}\) we do this by equating coefficients on both sides of the equation. We want to write both series in terms of \(x^{n}\) so that we can do this, so we need to shift the summation on the left: we replace \(n\) with \(n+1\) inside the sum and decrease the limit of summation \(n=1\) to \(n=0\) to get
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(n+1)c_{n+1}x^{n} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}4c_{n}x^{n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Now we can equate coefficients: for \(n\geq0\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} (n+1)c_{n+1} = 4c_{n}\qq{or} c_{n+1} = \frac{4}{n+1}c_{n}. \end{equation*}
    This is known as a recurrence relation. It describes the coefficients in terms of the previous ones, and can be used to determine explicitly what each \(c_{n}\) looks like. To see how, we plug several values for \(n\) into this recurrence relation to try to determine a pattern:
    \begin{align*} c_{1} &= \frac{4}{2}c_{0}\\ c_{2} &= \frac{4}{3}c_{1} = \frac{4^{2}}{3\cdot2}c_{0}\\ c_{3} &= \frac{4}{4}c_{2} = \frac{4^{3}}{4\cdot3\cdot2}c_{0} \end{align*}
    and in general it appears that
    \begin{equation*} c_{n} = \frac{4^{n}}{n!}c_{0} \end{equation*}
    for each \(n\text{.}\)
    We can‚Äôt solve for \(c_{0}\) without specifying an initial condition of some kind.
    So the solution \(y\) is
    \begin{align*} y &= \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}c_{n}x^{n}\\ &= \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{4^{n}}{n!}c_{0}x^{n}\\ &= c_{0}\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(4x)^{n}}{n!}\\ &= c_{0}e^{4x}\text{.} \end{align*}
    To solve the above ODE we used the following steps:
    1. Write \(y = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}c_{n}x^{n}\text{.}\)
    2. Use the ODE to build a recurrence relation for the coefficients \(c_{n}\text{.}\)
    3. Find an explicit description of the coefficients.
    4. Identify \(y\) as the power series of some function.

    Example 5.1.7.

    Use power series to solve the ODE \(y^\prime+2xy=0\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    We will solve this using the steps listed above. First, assume \(y = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}c_{k}x^{k}\text{.}\) Now plug this guess for \(y\) into the ODE to get
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}kc_{k}x^{k-1} = -\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}2c_{k}x^{k+1}. \end{equation*}
    We want to equate coefficients to build a recurrence relation, so we need to rewrite these sums in terms of a single \(x^{n}\text{,}\) as opposed to \(x^{k-1}\) and \(x^{k+1}\text{.}\) We do this by shifting the sums, but we need to remember to shift the limits of each sum as well:
    Sum Index Limit
    \(\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}kc_{k}x^{k-1}\) \(k-1\to n\) & \(k=1\to n=0\)
    \(-\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}2c_{k}x^{k+1}\) \(k+1\to n\) & \(k=0\to n=1\)
    So we get
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(n+1)c_{n+1}x^{n} = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(-2)c_{n-1}x^{n} = 0. \end{equation*}
    So a recurrence relation for \(c_{n}\) is
    \begin{equation*} c_{n+1} = -\frac{2}{n+1}c_{n-1}\qq{for }. \end{equation*}
    Since this two-step recurrence relation is only valid for \(n\geq1\text{,}\) it places no restrictions on any constant terms of the series. We‚Äôll need to determine these separately. To do so, write out the first couple terms of the sums in the previous equation:
    \begin{equation*} c_{1}+2c_{2}x+\dots = -2c_{0}x -2c_{1}x^{2}+\cdots \end{equation*}
    This tells us that \(c_{1} = 0\text{.}\) Again, we can‚Äôt get this information from the recurrence relation!
    Now we try to find an explicit formula for \(c_{n}\text{.}\) Because this is a two-step recurrence, we will write out the coefficients in two columns, one for odd \(n\) and one for even \(n\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} c_{2} &= -\frac{2}{2}c_{0} & c_{3} &= -\frac{2}{3}c_{1} = 0\\ c_{4} &= -\frac{2}{4}c_{2} = \frac{1}{2}\frac{1}{1}c_{0} & c_{5} &= -\frac{2}{5}c_{3} = 0\\ c_{6} &= -\frac{2}{6}c_{4} = -\frac{1}{3}\frac{1}{2}\frac{1}{1}c_{0} & c_{7} &= -\frac{2}{7}c_{5} = 0\text{.} \end{align*}
    So it appears that
    \begin{equation*} c_{2k} = \frac{(-1)^{k}}{k!}c_{0}\qq{and}c_{2k+1} = 0 \end{equation*}
    for every \(k\text{.}\)
    Now we plug this into our power series for \(y\) to get
    \begin{align*} y &= \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}c_{k}x^{k}\\ &= \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}c_{2k}x^{2k}\\ &= \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}c_{0}\frac{(-1)^{k}}{k!}x^{2k}\\ &= c_{0}\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-x^{2})^{k}}{k!}\\ &= c_{0}e^{-x^{2}}\text{.} \end{align*}
    Now that we have an idea of how to solve differential equations using power series, it can be useful to know when this method is actually valid, i.e., when power series solutions exist. We will be particularly concerned with solutions of second-order linear ODEs.

    Theorem 5.1.8. Existence of Series Solutions.

    Consider the differential equation
    \begin{equation*} y'' + P(x)y^\prime + Q(x)y = R(x)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    If \(P(x), Q(x)\) and \(R(x)\) are analytic at a point \(x_{0}\text{,}\) then every solution is also analytic at \(x_{0}\text{.}\)

    Example 5.1.9. A Legendre Equation.

    Show that
    \begin{equation*} (1 - x^{2})y'' - 2xy^\prime + 2y = 0 \end{equation*}
    has a series solution centered at \(0\) and then find the solution up to the coefficient of \(x^{6}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    First, note that the equation can be rewritten
    \begin{equation*} y'' - \frac{2x}{1 - x^{2}}y^\prime + \frac{2}{1 - x^{2}}y = 0\text{,} \end{equation*}
    so we are guaranteed a series solution centered at \(x = 0\text{.}\) Furthermore, this solution has radius of convergence at least \(R = 1\text{.}\)
    To find the solution, we return to the original equation and substitute \(y = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}c_{n}x^{n}\) to get
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n\geq0}n(n-1)c_{n}x^{n-2} - \sum_{n\geq0}n(n-1)c_{n}x^{n} - \sum_{n\geq0}2nc_{n}x^{n} + \sum_{n\geq0}2c_{n}x^{n} = 0 \end{equation*}
    which becomes
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n\geq0}(n + 2)(n+1)c_{n+2}x^{n} - \sum_{n\geq0}n(n-1)c_{n}x^{n} - \sum_{n\geq0}2nc_{n}x^{n} + \sum_{n\geq0}2c_{n}x^{n} = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    After a little algebra, we get the recurrence relation
    \begin{equation*} c_{n+2} = \frac{n-1}{n+1}c_{n}\quad\text{for}\quad n\geq2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This recurrence is valid for \(n\geq0\text{.}\)
    Now we can use the recurrence to list the first several terms of the solution:
    \begin{equation*} y = c_{1}x + c_{0}(1 - x^{2} - \frac{1}{3}x^{4} - \frac{1}{5}x^{6} - \cdots) \end{equation*}
    In fact,
    \begin{equation*} y = c_{1}x - \sum_{n\geq0}\frac{1}{2n - 1}c_{0}x^{2n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
