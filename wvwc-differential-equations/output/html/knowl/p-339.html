Suppose that \(\vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_{1} \\ v_{2} \end{bmatrix}\) is an eigenvector corresponding to \(\lambda\text{.}\) Then we know that

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix}1 & 4 \\ 1 & 1\end{bmatrix}\vec{v} = \lambda\vec{v} \Rightarrow \begin{bmatrix}v_{1}+4v_{2} \\ v_{1} + v_{2}\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda v_{1} \\ \lambda v_{2} \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}

This tells us that if \(\vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix}v_{1} \\ v_{2} \end{bmatrix}\) is an eigenvector for \(\lambda\text{,}\) then its entries need to satisfy

\begin{align} v_{1}+4v_{2}& =\lambda v_{1}\notag\\ v_{1}+v_{2} & = \lambda v_{2}\notag \end{align}

which boils down to

\begin{align*} (1-\lambda)v_{1}+4v_{2} & = 0\\ v_{1} + (1-\lambda)v_{2} & = 0. \end{align*}
