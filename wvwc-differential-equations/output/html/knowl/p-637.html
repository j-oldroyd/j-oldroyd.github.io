Consider the function \(F(s)\) in the frequency domain \(s\) defined by
\begin{equation*} F(s) = \frac{1}{s(s^{2}+4)}. \end{equation*}
This does not match a Laplace transform on our table. If we wanted to find the inverse transform \(f(t)\text{,}\) we would have to use partial fractions to find it. However, \(F(s)\) is easily seen to be the product of two recognizable transforms:
\begin{equation*} F(s) = \frac{1}{s} \frac{1}{s^{2}+4} = \frac{1}{2}\Laplace{1}\Laplace{\sin2t}. \end{equation*}
What we would like to do is find a way to determine inverse transforms of products of transforms. To do this, we need to define the convolution of two functions.
