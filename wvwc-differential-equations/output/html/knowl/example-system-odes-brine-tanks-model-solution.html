Example 4.2.12. Determining salt concentration in connected tank system.

Two brine tanks are set up as in Figure 4.1.3. Fresh water flows into the tank at a rate of \(r_{1}\text{,}\) well-mixed solution flows from Tank 1 to Tank 2 at a rate of \(r_{2}\) and well-mixed solution flows out of Tank 2 at a rate of \(r_{3}\text{.}\) Suppose that \(r_{1}, r_{2}\) and \(r_{3}\) are 5 gal⁄min, the volume of solution in Tank 1 is 10 gal and the volume of solution in Tank 2 is 7 gal. Suppose Tank 1 has 5 lb of salt at time \(t=0\) and Tank 2 has 2 lb of salt at time \(t=0\text{.}\) How much salt is in each tank at time \(t\text{?}\)
Solution.
To start, let \(x_{1}(t)\) denote the amount of salt in Tank 1 at time \(t\) and \(x_{2}(t)\) denote the amount of salt in Tank 2 at time \(t\text{,}\) where \(t\) is in minutes. Then from Section 4.1, we know that
\begin{align*} x^\prime_{1} \amp = -\frac{1}{2}x_{1}\\ x^\prime_{2} \amp = \frac{1}{2}x_{1}-\frac{5}{7}x_{2} \end{align*}
If we set
\begin{equation*} \vec{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_{1} \\ x_{2} \end{bmatrix} \qq{and} A = \begin{bmatrix}-\frac{1}{2} \amp 0 \\ \frac{1}{2} \amp -\frac{5}{7}\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
then this system is equivalent to the matrix ODE \(\vec{x}^\prime = A\vec{x}\text{.}\)
To solve this, we find the eigenvalues and corresponding eigenvectors. To find the eigenvalues, we could solve the characteristic equation \(\det(A-\lambda I) = 0\) or use technology, but it’s easier to note that \(A\) is a triangular matrix. So the eigenvalues are just \(\lambda_{1} = -\frac{1}{2},\lambda_{2} = -\frac{5}{7}\text{.}\)
Now we find corresponding eigenvectors. So let
\begin{equation*} \vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix}v_{1} \\ v_{2}\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
If \(\vec{v}\) is an eigenvector for \(\lambda\text{,}\) then we know \(A\vec{v} = \lambda\vec{v}\text{,}\) which gives the system
\begin{align*} (-\frac{1}{2}-\lambda)v_{1} \amp = 0\\ \frac{1}{2}v_{1} + (-\frac{5}{7}-\lambda)v_{2} \amp = 0 \end{align*}
If we set \(\lambda=-\frac{1}{2}\text{,}\) then we just get \(v_{1} = \frac{3}{14}v_{2}\text{.}\) So an eigenvector corresponding to \(\lambda_{1} = -\frac{1}{2}\) is
\begin{equation*} \vec{v}_{1} = \begin{bmatrix}3\\14\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Similarly, if we set \(\lambda=-\frac{5}{7}\) we get \(v_{1} = 0\text{,}\) but no restrictions on \(v_{2}\text{.}\) So an eigenvector corresponding to \(\lambda_{2} = -\frac{5}{7}\) is
\begin{equation*} \vec{v}_{2} = \begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
We can now write down the general solution of the matrix ODE:
\begin{equation*} \vec{x} = c_{1}e^{-\frac{5t}{7}}\begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix} + c_{2}e^{-\frac{t}{2}}\begin{bmatrix}3\\14\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}3c_{2}e^{-\frac{t}{2}} \\ c_{1}e^{-\frac{5t}{7}}+14c_{2}e^{-\frac{t}{2}}\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
But we’re not done yet, since we have the initial conditions \(x_{1}(0) = 5\) and \(x_{2}(0) = 2\text{,}\) or in terms of our matrix ODE
\begin{equation*} \vec{x}(0) = \begin{bmatrix}5\\2\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
We can use this to find \(c_{1}\) and \(c_{2}\text{.}\) If we set \(t=0\) then we get
\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix}3c_{2} \\ c_{1} + 14c_{2}\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}5\\2\end{bmatrix}\text{,} \end{equation*}
so \(c_{2} = \frac{5}{3}\) and \(c_{1} = 2 - 14\frac{5}{3} = -\frac{64}{3}\text{.}\)
So the solution of the matrix ODE (and hence the original system) is
\begin{equation*} \vec{x} = \begin{bmatrix}5e^{-\frac{t}{2}} \\ -\frac{64}{3}e^{-\frac{5t}{7}}+\frac{70}{3}e^{-\frac{t}{2}}\end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
The amount of salt in the first tank, \(x_{1}\text{,}\) is given by the top entry and the amount of salt in the second tank, \(x_{2}\text{,}\) is given by the bottom entry.
