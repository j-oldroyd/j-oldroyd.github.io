Solution 1.3.4.1.

It’s tough to see what to do right away, so we’ll try simplifying the ODE first. In particular, we’ll solve for \(y^\prime\) to get
\begin{equation*} y^\prime = 1+\frac{y}{x}. \end{equation*}
If we stare at this for a while, we might convince ourselves that the right hand side really just depends on \(\frac{y}{x}\text{,}\) so we’ll try replacing that with \(u\text{.}\) Then the ODE becomes \(y^\prime=1+u\text{.}\)
Once again, this is much simpler but we need to rewrite \(y^\prime\) in terms of \(u\text{.}\) Since \(u=\frac{y}{x}\text{,}\) this means that \(y=ux\) and so \(y^\prime = u+u^\prime x\text{.}\) Then the ODE becomes
\begin{equation*} u+x\dv{u}{x} = 1+u\text{ or just } x\dv{u}{x} = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
This new ODE can be rearranged to get \(\dv{u}{x} = \frac{1}{x}\text{,}\) and so \(u = \ln|x|+C\text{.}\) Getting back in terms of \(y\text{,}\) we have \(\frac{y}{x} = \ln|x|+C\) or just \(y = x\ln|x|+Cx\text{.}\)
