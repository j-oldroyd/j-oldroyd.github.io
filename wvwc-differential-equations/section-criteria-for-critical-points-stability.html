Section 4.3 Criteria for Critical Points; Stability

Consider the matrix ODE \(\vec{y}' = A\vec{y}\text{.}\) Let \(\lambda_{1},\lambda_{2}\) denote the eigenvalues of the \(2\times 2\) matrix \(A\text{.}\) Then \(\vec{0}\) is a:

The rule of thumb is this: the real parts of the eigenvalues determine whether a trajectory moves towards or away from the origin, and the imaginary part determines if the trajectory has a periodic/oscillating nature to it.

We say that the origin is a stable critical point of \(\vec{y}'=A\vec{y}\) if all trajectories that start ``close'' to \(\vec{0}\) remain close at all future times. Equivalently, it's stable if each trajectory will eventually be contained within some circle centered at the origin as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\) Otherwise, we say that \(\vec{0}\) is unstable. If it so happens that every trajectory that starts close to \(\vec{0}\) tends to \(\vec{0}\) as \(t\to\infty\text{,}\) we then say that \(\vec{0}\) is a stable and attractive (or asymptotically stable) critical point. Equivalently, \(\vec{0}\) is asymptotically stable if every trajectory goes to \(\vec{0}\) as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\)

Example 4.3.2.

Let \(\vec{y}' = A\vec{y}\) denote a matrix ODE where \(A\) is a constant \(2\times2\) matrix. What conditions on the eigenvalues of \(A\) will give an asymptotically stable critical point at \(\vec{y}=\vec{0}\text{?}\)

Solution

Let \(\vec{y} = \vec{y}(t)\) denote a nonzero solution of the matrix ODE (and therefore a trajectory). Then in order for \(\vec{0}\) to be asymptotically stable, we need \(\vec{y}\to\vec{0}\) as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\) Let \(\lambda_{1},\lambda_{2}\) denote the eigenvalues of \(A\text{.}\) Then \(\vec{y}\) will have the form

\begin{equation*} \vec{y} = c_{1}e^{\lambda_{1}t}\vec{y}_{1}+c_{2}e^{\lambda_{2}t}\vec{y}_{2}. \end{equation*}

This will go to \(\vec{0}\) as \(t\to\infty\) if either \(c_{1}=c_{2}=0\) or if each exponential goes to \(0\) as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\) Since we assume \(\vec{y}\neq\vec{0}\text{,}\) this means we need \(e^{\lambda_{i}t}\to0\) for \(i=1,2\) as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\) This means that the real part of each eigenvalue must be negative. This is because the real part of each eigenvalue determines the growth of \(e^{\lambda_{i}t}\text{:}\) if \(\lambda_{i} = a+bi\text{,}\) then

\begin{equation*} e^{\lambda_{i}t} = e^{at}[A\cos bt+B\sin bt]\text{.} \end{equation*}

So \(\vec{0}\) is asymptotically stable if the real parts of both eigenvalues are negative.

Similarly, we can say that \(\vec{0}\) is stable as long as the real part of each eigenvalue is no greater than \(0\text{.}\) \(\vec{0}\) is unstable if the real part of \emph{any} eigenvalue is positive.

Example 4.3.3.

Two tanks \(T_{1}\) and \(T_{2}\) containing 200 gal each of a water-salt mixture are set up as follows:

  • Tank 1: Pure water flows in at 12 gal⁄min and solution from Tank 2 flows in at 4 gal⁄min; solution also flows out of Tank 1 and into Tank 2 at 16 gal⁄min.

  • Solution from Tank 1 flows in at 16 gal⁄min; solution flows out of Tank 2 and into Tank 1 at 4 gal⁄min, and solution is emptied from Tank 2 at an addition rate of 12 gal⁄min

Will the salt eventually empty from both tanks?

Solution

Let \(y_{1}(t)\) denote the amount of salt (in pounds) in Tank 1 at time \(t\) (in minutes), and let \(y_{2}(t)\) do the same for Tank 2. Then

\begin{align*} y'_{1} \amp = 4\frac{y_{2}}{200} - 16\frac{y_{1}}{200}\\ y'_{2} \amp = 16\frac{y_{1}}{200} - 16\frac{y_{2}}{200}\text{.} \end{align*}

This is equivalent to the matrix ODE \(\vec{y}' = A\vec{y}\) where

\begin{equation*} \vec{y} = \begin{bmatrix}y_{1}\\y_{2}\end{bmatrix}\quad\text{and}\quad A = \begin{bmatrix}-\frac{2}{25} \amp \frac{1}{50} \\ \frac{2}{25} \amp -\frac{2}{25}\end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}

The eigenvalues of \(A\) are

\begin{equation*} \lambda_{1} = -\frac{1}{25}\quad\text{and}\quad\lambda_{2} = -\frac{3}{25}. \end{equation*}

Since both eigenvalues have negative real part, it follows that \(\vec{0}\) is an asymptotically stable critical point of \(\vec{y}'=A\vec{y}\text{.}\) Therefore every trajectory \(\vec{y}\to\vec{0}\) as \(t\to\infty\text{.}\) So no matter how much salt is initially in the tanks, the amount of salt will always go to \(0\text{.}\)
